## Brand Overview

### Therabody (Theragun)

Therabody launched the original Theragun in 2016 and essentially created the consumer percussive therapy category. Founded by chiropractor Dr. Jason Wersland, the company has positioned itself as the premium, science-backed option. Its current lineup — the Theragun mini, Theragun Sense, and Theragun PRO Plus — spans from $199 to $499. Therabody also expanded into pneumatic compression (RecoveryAir), hot/cold therapy (RecoveryTherm), and CBD topicals.

### Hyperice (Hypervolt)

Hyperice entered the market in 2017 and quickly became Therabody's primary competitor. The brand is known for partnerships with the NFL, NBA, and UFC, and its focus on accessible, user-friendly design. The current Hypervolt lineup includes the Hypervolt Go 2, Hypervolt 2, and Hypervolt 2 Pro, priced from $129 to $329. Hyperice also makes the Normatec compression boots and Venom heat wraps.

Both brands have a strong clinical and professional sports presence, but they take different approaches: Therabody leans into amplitude and raw percussive depth, while Hyperice prioritizes quiet operation and ergonomic simplicity.

## Percussion Power & Amplitude

This is the single biggest technical differentiator between the two brands.

### Amplitude

Amplitude measures how far the massage head travels back and forth — essentially, how deep the percussion reaches into muscle tissue.

- **Theragun PRO Plus: 16 mm amplitude.** This is the deepest amplitude in any mainstream consumer massage gun. It reaches well past the superficial fascia and into the belly of large muscle groups like the glutes, quads, and erector spinae.
- **Hypervolt 2 Pro: 14 mm amplitude.** Still a solid figure, and enough for effective myofascial release and muscle recovery. The difference between 14 mm and 16 mm is noticeable on thick muscle groups but less so on smaller areas like the forearms or calves.

### Stall Force

Stall force measures how much pressure you can apply before the motor stalls out and stops percussing.

- **Theragun PRO Plus: Up to 60 lbs.** You can press hard into dense tissue without the device slowing down. This matters for therapists working on large, muscular clients and for targeting deep knots.
- **Hypervolt 2 Pro: Up to 35 lbs.** Sufficient for most self-treatment and general recovery, but it can bog down under heavy pressure on very dense tissue.

### Percussions Per Minute (PPM)

- **Theragun PRO Plus:** 1,750–2,400 PPM across five speeds. The lower floor is intentional — slow, deep percussion is effective for parasympathetic activation and gentle warm-ups.
- **Hypervolt 2 Pro:** 1,800–3,200 PPM across three speeds. The higher ceiling provides a faster, more vibratory sensation at the top speed setting, which some users prefer for surface-level flush work.

**Bottom line:** If deep-tissue treatment is your priority — especially for large muscle groups or professional use — the Theragun's 16 mm amplitude and 60 lb stall force give it a clear edge. For general recovery and lighter use, the Hypervolt's 14 mm amplitude is more than adequate.

## Noise Level

Noise is consistently one of the top complaints about percussive therapy devices, and this is where Hyperice has historically outperformed Therabody.

- **Hypervolt 2 Pro:** Rated around 53–64 dB(A) depending on speed and attachment. At its lowest setting, it's roughly the volume of a quiet conversation. Hyperice uses a brushless motor with patented QuietGlide technology that noticeably dampens operational noise.
- **Theragun PRO Plus:** Rated around 60–70 dB(A). Therabody has made significant strides since the notoriously loud original Theragun, but the deeper amplitude inherently produces more noise. At full speed with a standard ball attachment, it's roughly comparable to a loud conversation or a running dishwasher.

The difference is noticeable in shared spaces. If you plan to use your massage gun in an office, a shared apartment, or while watching TV, the Hypervolt is meaningfully quieter. If you primarily use the device in a gym, garage, or treatment room, the Theragun's noise level is a non-issue.

**Bottom line:** Hypervolt wins on noise. If quiet operation matters to you, this is a genuine advantage.

## Battery Life & Charging

### Theragun PRO Plus

- **Battery life:** Approximately 150 minutes on a single charge (varies by speed and pressure).
- **Battery type:** Removable lithium-ion. This is a significant advantage for professionals — you can swap a dead battery for a fresh one in seconds. Therabody sells extra batteries separately.
- **Charging:** Proprietary charging stand. Charges in approximately 75 minutes.

### Hypervolt 2 Pro

- **Battery life:** Approximately 180 minutes per charge.
- **Battery type:** Internal (non-removable). Slightly longer overall runtime, but when it's dead, the device is out of commission until it recharges.
- **Charging:** USB-C. This is a major convenience advantage — you can charge it with the same cable you use for your laptop, phone, or other devices. No proprietary charger to lose.

**Bottom line:** For home users, the Hypervolt's longer battery life and USB-C charging are more convenient. For professionals running back-to-back sessions, the Theragun's swappable battery is a game-changer.

## Design & Ergonomics

### Theragun PRO Plus

The Theragun uses a distinctive triangular handle design with a multi-grip ergonomic shape. This allows four different grip positions:

1. **Standard grip** (vertical)
2. **Angled grip** (45-degree)
3. **Horizontal grip** (for pressing into the body)
4. **Reverse grip** (for reaching the back and shoulders)

This multi-grip system is the Theragun's signature and is genuinely useful for self-treatment of hard-to-reach areas, especially the upper back and posterior shoulder. However, the triangular shape makes the device bulkier and harder to pack in a standard gym bag.

**Weight:** 2.8 lbs (1.27 kg)

### Hypervolt 2 Pro

The Hypervolt uses a traditional pistol-grip (T-shape) design. It's more compact and intuitive to pick up and use immediately — there's no learning curve. The single handle orientation is comfortable for most use cases but can create wrist fatigue when treating your own back or the opposite-side shoulder.

**Weight:** 2.6 lbs (1.18 kg)

**Bottom line:** The Theragun's multi-grip design is more versatile for self-treatment. The Hypervolt's pistol grip is simpler and more portable. If you primarily treat yourself and value reach, Theragun. If you value compactness and simplicity, Hypervolt.

**Bottom line:** Therabody's app gives more manual control. Hyperice's app provides smarter automated recommendations. Both are among the best companion apps in the recovery device market.

## FAQ

### Is the Theragun really worth $499?

For professionals and serious athletes, yes. The combination of 16 mm amplitude, 60 lbs of stall force, swappable batteries, and on-device force display provides capabilities that no other consumer device matches. For casual users, the Theragun Sense ($299) or Hypervolt 2 Pro ($329) offers a better value proposition.

### Are massage guns actually effective, or are they just hype?

Research supports their use. A 2020 study in the *Journal of Clinical and Diagnostic Research* found that percussive therapy significantly reduced delayed-onset muscle soreness (DOMS). A 2021 review in the *Journal of Sports Science & Medicine* concluded that vibration and percussion devices improve short-term range of motion and reduce perceived muscle pain. They are not a replacement for professional manual therapy, stretching, or adequate sleep — but they are a legitimate recovery tool.

### Can I use a massage gun on my neck and spine?

Use extreme caution. Never apply a massage gun directly to the vertebrae, the front or sides of the neck (where carotid arteries run), or any bony prominence. You can use a massage gun on the muscles beside the spine (the erector spinae group) and the upper trapezius using a soft attachment at low speed. The Hypervolt's fork attachment is specifically designed for safe paraspinal use. When in doubt, consult a physiotherapist.

### How long should I use a massage gun on each muscle group?

Clinical guidelines generally recommend 30 seconds to 2 minutes per muscle group. Spending more than 2 minutes on one area can cause bruising, nerve irritation, or increased inflammation. Start at a lower speed and shorter duration, then increase gradually based on tolerance.

### Which is quieter, Theragun or Hypervolt?

The Hypervolt is noticeably quieter at every comparable speed setting. The Hypervolt 2 Pro operates at approximately 53–64 dB(A), while the Theragun PRO Plus operates at approximately 60–70 dB(A). The gap narrows when both devices are used at their lowest settings.

### Can I take a massage gun on a plane?

Yes, both Theragun and Hypervolt devices are TSA-approved for carry-on luggage (lithium-ion batteries must be in carry-on, not checked baggage). The Theragun mini and Hypervolt Go 2 are the most travel-friendly options due to their compact size. Always check your specific airline's policies before traveling internationally.

### Do Theragun and Hypervolt attachments work with each other's devices?

No. The attachment mechanisms are proprietary and not cross-compatible. Each brand's attachments only fit their own devices.

### How often should you replace a massage gun?

With normal use (15–30 minutes per day), both Theragun and Hypervolt devices typically last 3–5 years before battery degradation or motor wear becomes noticeable. Both brands offer a 2-year warranty. Replacement batteries for the Theragun extend the device's usable lifespan significantly.
